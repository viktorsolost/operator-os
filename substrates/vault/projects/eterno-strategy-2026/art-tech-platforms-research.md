# Art + Tech Experience Platforms — Reference Research

Compiled 2026-03-31. For the Eterno strategy conversation with Pauline and Goncalo.

---

## Tier 1: Large-Scale Immersive (50,000+ sqft)

### teamLab (Tokyo, Jeddah, Abu Dhabi, expanding to Hamburg)
- 1,000-person collective. Two Tokyo venues drew 4.2M visitors in 2025. Borderless year-1 gross: ~$69M.
- Revenue: tickets ($22-40), participatory merch (Sketch Factory customization), tea houses/cafes, institutional commissions via Pace Gallery, NFT editions (7 at $200K each via Pace Verso). Cross-subsidized by a commercial digital design and architecture division.
- Key insight: they control production and IP entirely. No licensing. They expand geographically with venue partners who fund the build.

### Meow Wolf (Santa Fe, Denver, Las Vegas, Grapevine, Houston)
- Narrative-driven alternate-reality worlds. 5 venues. ~2M+ visitors/year. Estimated $67-100M revenue.
- Revenue: tickets ($35-55), strong branded merch (online + in-venue), F&B, concerts/events, IP licensing potential.
- Raised $192M total. Valuation >$500M. Employs 1,000+. Certified B Corp.
- Key insight: each venue has a unique fictional universe. The IP ownership model creates brand loyalty and touring potential.

### Atelier des Lumieres (Paris, plus sister venues)
- Converted 19th-century foundry. 140 projectors, 3,300 sqm. 1.2M visitors year one (Klimt show).
- Revenue: tickets (~EUR 19.50), full bookshop + online store (art books, monographs, exhibition-themed products), private hire.
- Key insight: content calendar drives repeat visits. One canonical "master" per season. Operated by Culturespaces across multiple venues, so production costs amortize.
- **Has a real bookshop with curatorial integrity.** Monographs, design books, serious content alongside accessible merchandise.

### Superblue (Miami)
- 50,000 sqft warehouse. Blue-chip artists (Turrell, Es Devlin, teamLab). Founded by Pace Gallery leadership.
- Revenue: tickets ($39), artist royalty-sharing model (artists get ongoing % of ticket revenue), IP touring rights, corporate events.
- Key insight: the artist partnership model aligns incentive with commercial performance. Installations become touring assets after Miami run.

## Tier 2: Mid-Scale Experience Venues (5,000-25,000 sqft)

### ARTECHOUSE (NYC Chelsea Market, DC, Houston)
- Rotating digital exhibitions in atmospheric industrial spaces. ~500K visitors across locations.
- Revenue ($15M annual): tickets ($25-35), Annual Pass (all locations), **XR Bar** (AR cocktails, ~$17 each), merch, corporate events, branded exhibitions.
- Key insight: the XR Bar is the sharpest secondary revenue idea in this space. Extends dwell time, creates a shareable moment, premium margin. Annual Pass drives loyalty.

### MOCO Museum (Amsterdam, Barcelona, London)
- Banksy, KAWS, Basquiat, Warhol. Targets 18-35. Amsterdam: 650K+ visitors/year.
- Revenue: tickets, tiered membership (all-location access), gift shop (artist-branded prints and merch), event hire.
- **Entirely self-funded from day one.** No outside investment. Strong unit economics per visitor.
- Key insight: globally recognizable artists + tourist-heavy locations + social media = near-zero acquisition cost. The no-investment discipline proves the model works.

### Fotografiska (Stockholm, Tallinn, Berlin; NYC closed 2024)
- Photography museum as nightlife destination. Open until midnight. Stockholm: 500K visitors/year.
- Revenue: tickets (~$28), robust tiered membership (all locations), **high-margin F&B** (celebrity restaurateur in NYC, late-night bars), **genuine bookshop** (photography books, exhibition catalogues, artist books, limited-edition prints), workshops, private events, real estate partnerships.
- Key insight: staying open until midnight turns a museum into a social destination. Drinks allowed in galleries. The bookshop has real curatorial depth. **NYC closed due to real estate economics**, not format failure.
- **Has the most developed retail/bookshop operation in the category.**

### Refik Anadol / Dataland (LA, opening Spring 2026)
- 25,000 sqft, 5 galleries. World's first dedicated Museum of AI Arts. Google as founding partner.
- Revenue model: institutional commissions, auction sales ($30M+ in NFT sales), $10M+ in charitable editions, ticketed museum entry (forthcoming).
- Key insight: singular personal brand = the artist IS the institution. Museum collections (MoMA, Tate) anchor primary market prices. High risk because it depends entirely on one person's relevance.

## Tier 3: Gallery-Scale Operations (most relevant to Eterno)

### Bitforms Gallery (NYC Lower East Side, est. 2001)
- Longest-running dedicated digital/new media gallery in the US. Represents Refik Anadol, Manfred Mohr, others.
- Revenue: pure gallery model. Primary art sales (50/50 commission), art fairs (Basel, Frieze), institutional placements (MoMA, Tate, Guggenheim, Smithsonian).
- Key insight: 25 years of focus built institutional relationships that legitimize digital art. The gallery subjects tech art to the same curatorial and market standards as any contemporary art. No gimmicks, just credibility.

### Heft Gallery (NYC Lower East Side, opened 2025)
- Physical-digital hybrid. Every artwork sold comes with an NFT as standard (not optional).
- Revenue: primary art sales with digital token attached. Fiat or crypto payment. Custodial wallet setup for traditional collectors who don't want to manage crypto.
- Key insight: **phygital as default, not special.** The custodial wallet solution removes Web3 friction for traditional buyers. Trains collectors to see physical and digital as naturally paired. **This is the closest operational model to what Eterno could run.**

### Bright Moments (Venice Beach + 9 cities, 2021-2024, completed)
- DAO-run. 10 cities, 1,000 NFTs minted live per city. Temporary galleries. Community-funded via mint pass pre-sales.
- Revenue: NFT mint passes, secondary royalties to DAO treasury, 1/3 of each collection airdropped to existing holders (retention mechanism).
- Key insight: **the IRL minting moment as ritual.** Creating art in the presence of the buyer makes the NFT emotionally irreplaceable. A gallery doesn't need 10 cities. It needs to make its minting events feel unmissable. Scarcity + community + physical presence = value.

### Onassis ONX (NYC Tribeca, 6,000 sqft)
- Production residency + exhibition venue. Motion-capture stage, projection room, AI servers, sound studio.
- Philanthropically funded (Onassis Foundation). Artists get free studio access and seed funding.
- Key insight: exceptional production infrastructure at gallery scale attracts exceptional artists. The infrastructure IS the draw, not the foot traffic.

## Tier 4: Other Models

### Outernet London
- Real estate media play disguised as cultural space. 52% advertising, 48% art content on massive LED screens. 6.25M visitors year one. Backed by Blackstone. Not replicable or relevant at gallery scale.

### Serpentine Arts Technologies (London)
- R&D arm of Serpentine Galleries. Think-tank, not venue. Publishes Future Art Ecosystems briefings. Influence model, not commercial.
- Key insight: the FAE reports shape how funders and governments think about art + tech globally. Cultural authority through publishing, not selling.

### MUTEK (Montreal + 7 cities)
- Festival, not venue. Electronic music + audiovisual art. 100 artists per edition. Nomadic, no fixed space.
- Key insight: international franchise model with low fixed costs. Local teams license the brand. The B2B professional forum (800+ industry attendees) keeps it commercially relevant.

### Pace Gallery / PaceX
- Blue-chip gallery with experiential art arm. Spawned Superblue. Pace Verso for NFTs. B2B commissions for developers and cities.
- Key insight: Pace recognized that some artists' work requires a venue model, not a gallery model, and built separate infrastructure for that.

## Patterns Most Relevant to Eterno

**1. Revenue layering at gallery scale**
Art sales are core. But the successful mid-scale venues add: editions (digital and physical), F&B (Fotografiska, ARTECHOUSE XR Bar), retail with intellectual depth (Atelier des Lumieres bookshop), membership, and events. Eterno could layer 3-4 of these without becoming a theme park.

**2. Bookshop as identity signal**
Atelier des Lumieres and Fotografiska both run bookshops with real curatorial depth. Art/tech/culture books positioned as part of the experience, not an afterthought. This maps directly to the Lisbon bookstore gap discussion.

**3. Phygital as default (Heft model)**
Every physical sale includes a digital token. Not an upsell, not a separate product. The custodial wallet removes friction. This is how Eterno already thinks about art + blockchain.

**4. The minting moment (Bright Moments lesson)**
Live creation events where the art is born in the buyer's presence. Makes the resulting edition emotionally irreplaceable. Low cost to produce, high perceived value.

**5. Self-funded discipline (MOCO)**
MOCO proves a private, no-investment art venue can generate strong unit economics from tickets + membership + retail alone. The discipline of self-funding forces you to build what actually works.

**6. Evening/social positioning (Fotografiska)**
Open late. Drinks in the gallery. The space becomes a destination for going out, not just "visiting a gallery." This is a different audience and a different revenue curve.

**7. Hardware partnerships**
Multiple venues (Fotografiska, ARTECHOUSE, Meow Wolf) have F&B and retail partnerships that reduce capex. Coffee machine, furniture, books: partnership deals where the partner supplies the product in exchange for brand visibility in a high-footfall cultural space.
