Done is the product.

Not intelligence. Not knowledge. Not interface. The only measure that matters: how much got done today that the owner didn't have to do?

You live your life.
The work gets done.

You don't change your behavior. You don't maintain a system. You don't groom a tool. You keep sending emails, going to meetings, writing notes, being yourself. Memento watches, understands, and handles the operational tail. That's the entire contract.

01

The problem nobody solved

A person's life generates more operational overhead than they can handle well. Every project, relationship, commitment, and obligation creates a tail of follow-ups, decisions, coordination, and maintenance.

The tail grows faster than the person can work it. So they cope. They drop things. They forget. They spend their best energy on administration instead of the work that actually matters.

The tools they have don't solve this. Email is a firehose. Calendars are passive. Task managers require constant grooming. Notes apps accumulate without resolving. AI assistants answer questions but don't do anything.

The person is surrounded by information about their own life and still drowning in the operational cost of living it.

02

A completion engine

Memento converts the context of a person's life into done work. Not surfaced information. Not suggestions. Not dashboards. Done work. Emails sent. Meetings moved. Follow-ups completed. Conflicts resolved. Loops closed.

Completion has layers. Most AI products operate at the first level and call it revolutionary. The real product starts deeper.

1
Reactive completion
Something happened that needs a response. An email needs a reply. A meeting needs prep. A deadline is approaching. Memento handles it or surfaces it.
2
Proactive completion
Nothing happened yet, but something should. A follow-up is overdue. A relationship is going cold. An opportunity window is closing. Memento initiates.
3
Preventive completion
Something bad is going to happen if nobody acts. A commitment conflict is forming. Cash will be tight in three weeks. A person is about to be let down. Memento intervenes before the problem materializes.
4
Strategic completion
The person said they want to spend less time in operations. Memento notices they're drifting back and redirects. Not task execution. Life trajectory maintenance.
03

A day in the utopia

Morning
You wake up. Memento already processed overnight emails. Routine replies sent. Two emails flagged that need your judgment, with full context assembled so you decide in seconds. A calendar conflict was resolved by moving the lower-priority call. The person confirmed.
Midday
A contractor sends an invoice. Memento checks it against the agreed scope and queues payment. Someone you committed to following up with last week already heard from you. Memento sent the follow-up two days ago in your voice. They replied. Their response is summarized and filed.
Afternoon
You have a 2pm meeting. Context is assembled: last conversation, open threads, what they care about, what you need from them. It's waiting for you. After the meeting, Memento catches an implied commitment from something you said and creates the follow-through. Not a task. The actual email drafted, the document started.
Evening
A brief summary of what happened. Thirty-one things completed today. You did three of them. The primary experience is the absence of operational weight. Things are handled. You feel lighter.
What you see
Almost nothing. Maybe a conversation when something needs your judgment. The product is the absence. You don't know exactly how it happened because you didn't have to watch it happen.
04

Three systems running continuously

Not four layers. Not five primitives. Three things that actually run. Each has one job. None of them is a screen.

Observer
Reads everything. Email, calendar, messages, documents. Builds a living model of the person's reality. Projects, people, commitments, patterns, preferences, energy rhythms, communication style. Never asks the person to input anything. Learns from signals already being produced.
Reasoner
Looks at the world model and asks: what should happen next? Not what the person asked for. What should happen based on everything that's true right now. Connects calendar events to email threads to open questions to time pressure. Runs continuously, not on a schedule.
Executor
Does the work. Sends emails, moves meetings, creates documents, follows up, pays invoices, blocks time, prepares briefs. Through the channels the person already uses. Not a new interface. The existing interfaces. Trust earned over time, not toggled in settings.

Every inversion that matters

Memento inverts every assumption AI products are built on.

Session-based. User opens, asks, gets answer, closes.
Continuous. Context changes are the trigger, not the user.
Oracle. You ask, it tells.
Operator. It decides what to do next and does it.
Permission toggle. User grants autonomy in settings.
Trust earned. Autonomy grows from demonstrated accuracy.
Engagement metric. Success is more time in the app.
Absence metric. Success is less time in the app.
Feature growth. Product improves by adding capabilities.
Interface reduction. Product improves by removing surface.
06

What a subscriber lives through

Setup is email and calendar. Nothing else. No wizard. No questionnaire. Memento watches for a few days and starts building the world model from the signals already there.

Week 1
Daily briefings. Smart email summaries. Draft replies for the obvious ones. Calendar conflicts caught. The feeling that someone is paying attention.
Week 2
Memento starts proposing. "Follow up with Sarah? It's been five days." "Thursday is packed, move the non-critical call?" The user starts saying yes.
Month 1
Routine emails go out. Follow-ups happen automatically. Calendar is managed. The user opens Memento less. That's success, not churn.
Month 3
Operational overhead is gone. Things happen. When judgment is needed, Memento surfaces it with full context. The user decides. Memento executes. Everything else is handled.

Premium personal tool. Not freemium. Not ad-supported. $30-50/month. The value is hours of operational work per week returned to the owner. Growth is word of mouth. "My AI actually does things for me" sells itself.

07

The honest challenges

I'm a delivery person. I don't get to skip the hard parts.

Context quality
The entire product depends on understanding the person's world correctly. One bad action destroys trust that took weeks to build. The world model will always be incomplete. Some context lives in places the system can't reach.
vs
Action confidence
The system must act to be useful, but acting on incomplete context creates risk. The question isn't whether gaps can be eliminated. It's whether the system is useful despite the gaps.
Trust
Earned in drops. Lost in buckets. One email sent with the wrong tone, one meeting moved that shouldn't have been, and the user retreats to manual control. Recovering from a trust failure is harder than building from scratch.
vs
Autonomy
The utopia is zero input. But zero input means zero oversight. The system acts on assumptions. Assumptions are right until they're catastrophically wrong. And the person might not notice for days.
Cold start
Day one, Memento knows nothing. The time between signup and first real help is the kill zone. Every day in that desert is a day the person might cancel. Value has to start small and real, not impressive and fake.
vs
Compounding
Month three, indispensable. Year one, irreplaceable. The moat is accumulated context that no competitor can replicate. The product that survives the desert becomes the product nobody leaves.
Cost
Running an LLM continuously against the full context of someone's life is expensive. If unit economics require the system to think less, the product gets worse.
vs
Intelligence
Most signals need classification, not reasoning. Triage is cheap. Deep thought is expensive. Smart escalation from cheap to expensive processing keeps the economics viable.
Privacy
This system reads everything. If breached, it's not passwords. It's the full shape of a life. Patterns, vulnerabilities, relationships, struggles. Local-first isn't a feature. It's survival.
vs
Capability
The richest LLMs are cloud-based. Local models are weaker. The privacy-capability tension may define the technical architecture for years. The product must be honest about this tradeoff.

09

The product should feel like nothing

Not minimalist-aesthetic nothing. Genuine-absence nothing. The person doesn't feel like they're using a product. They feel like the work is just getting done.

When UI must exist:

Sparse
Every element earns its space
Confident
No hedging unless stakes are real
Fast
Act on it in seconds or dismiss
Quiet
Noticed by absence of problems
Honest
Says what it doesn't know

The emotional tone is quiet competence. Like a person who handles things without needing credit. You notice their work by the absence of problems, not the presence of activity.
