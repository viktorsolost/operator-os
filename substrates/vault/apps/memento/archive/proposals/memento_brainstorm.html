Anton Proposal

Core Thesis

Memento should be the stripped, correct version of VIK OS. Not dashboard first. Not team software. Not a workspace layer for companies. A personal companion built on one simple contract: I maintain truth. Memento handles the rest.

What Memento Is

  • A personal operator
  • A context-driven system, not a prompt-driven system
  • A bridge between source of truth and action
  • An autonomy system designed to reduce owner input toward zero
  • A trust product before it is a feature product

What Memento Is Not

  • Not a dashboard product
  • Not a task manager
  • Not a notes app
  • Not a CRM with AI attached
  • Not company collaboration software

Product Definition

Memento is an autonomous personal chief of staff. It continuously reads the owner's trusted context, decides what matters, makes plans, does the work it can do safely, and asks for input only when confidence is low, permissions are required, or judgment is deeply personal.

This is not an app with AI features. It is a context engine with an execution loop.

The Core Loop

  1. Read truth
  2. Resolve context
  3. Infer what matters now
  4. Plan the next useful moves
  5. Execute what is safe
  6. Ask only when needed
  7. Learn from corrections
  8. Repeat

Core Primitives

People

The relationship layer.

Projects

The active containers of work and intention.

Commitments

Promises, deadlines, meetings, obligations.

Context

Notes, documents, messages, evidence, meaning.

Actions

The next moves Memento can plan or perform.

Architecture Direction

Truth Layer

The connected sources where reality already exists.

Context Layer

The interpreted meaning graph, who matters, what is active, what is blocked, what is risky, what is waiting.

Execution Layer

The agentic loop that drafts, sends, schedules, follows up, prepares, updates, and closes loops.

Trust Layer

The policy boundary for acting silently, notifying, asking, or refusing.

Design Commandments

  1. No duplicate truth entry.
  2. No manual project management unless unavoidable.
  3. No UI surface unless conversation and automation fail.

Trust Model

  • Safe to do silently
  • Safe to do with passive notification
  • Needs approval
  • Never autonomous

Utopia Model, Ignoring VIK OS

The clean version of Memento is not a product made of pages. It is a loop with a thin control surface.

Small UI

  • Conversation
  • What Memento is handling now
  • Approvals
  • Memory and preferences
  • Audit trail

Pros

  • Aligned with product truth
  • Avoids feature sprawl
  • Easier to explain
  • Can become genuinely autonomous

Cons / Hard Parts

  • Context quality is everything
  • Trust is fragile
  • Personal data is messy
  • Sustained correctness is hard
  • Zero-input autonomy is asymptotic

Returning To VIK OS, What UI Survives

Keep

  • Conversation
  • Approvals
  • Handling Now / agenda
  • Context inspect
  • Audit trail
  • Settings / trust controls

Cut Or Compress

  • Deep navigation structures
  • Standalone dashboards
  • Most status surfaces
  • List-heavy admin pages
  • Project pages restating existing data
  • Milestone surfaces requiring manual upkeep

Survival test: a screen survives only if it materially improves truth, context resolution, execution, or trust.

Minimum viable shell: Chat, Today / Handling Now, Approvals, Context Inspect, History.

Blunt view: if VIK OS needs a lot of UI to feel usable, that is evidence the context-to-action loop is still weak.

You don't get to zero input by making the AI smarter. You get there by making the context so rich that the AI doesn't need to ask.

01
Cognitive Extension, Not Assistant

An assistant waits for instructions. A cognitive extension shares your intent. It knows what you are trying to achieve across every domain of your life and it is always working the problem, not waiting to be asked.

Nobody has built this because everyone frames the problem wrong. They start from the tool layer: what can we automate? Memento starts from the identity layer: who is this person? What do they care about? What are they building? What patterns do they repeat that they cannot see?

02
The Desert

Day one, Memento knows nothing. This is where every AI product dies. The gap between empty tool and useful companion is a desert most people will not cross.

The only way across: earn context without burdening the user. Observe. Not surveil. Connect to the surfaces where life already happens. Email, calendar, messages, files, notes. Change nothing. Watch the streams. Interpret, do not store.

She emails Sarah a lot. Sarah is on Project X. Project X has an April deadline. They have not discussed deliverables in two weeks. That is context. The raw emails are just data.

03
Earning Trust

Permission is earned through accuracy over time. Not granted through a toggle. The system does not ask for access. It demonstrates competence until the user says just handle it.

Observe

Silent. Building the model. No output. No interruptions.

Reflect

Here is what I think your world looks like. Am I right? Corrections reveal what you care about, not just what you do.

Anticipate

This thread is going stale. This deadline is three days out and the deliverable is not ready.

Propose

A draft based on how you have handled similar situations. Move this meeting, it conflicts with your deep work.

Act

Sent that reply. You have approved forty like it. This one was routine.

04
The Web, Not The Filing Cabinet

Most AI memory stores facts. Name, role, last interaction. That is a contact book. Real context is a living weighted graph of meaning. Everything connects to everything.

meeting → negotiation → project behind schedule → slow contractor → payment dispute → cash flow → deal closing next week

Humans navigate this web intuitively but imperfectly. We forget threads, miss connections, react instead of anticipating. Memento holds the entire web. Never drops a thread. Never fails to see that a decision in one area ripples into three others.

05
Almost No Interface

You speak, it responds with the full weight of your context behind it. No navigation. No pages. No dashboards.

Three secondary surfaces exist only when conversation fails: approvals, activity feed, context corrections. If you need more UI than that, the context engine is not good enough yet.

06
Compound Interest

Year one, useful. Year five, indispensable. Year ten, it knows you better than anyone in your life.

Not because it stored more facts. Because the web got denser, the patterns clearer, the trust deeper. It learned not just what you do but why. Not your schedule but your rhythms. Not your contacts but your relationships.

You are not paying for features. You are paying for continuity. The longer you stay, the more it is worth.

07
What Is Real And What Is Hard
Works
  • No behavior change required
  • Trust builds organically
  • Value compounds over time
  • Natural subscription curve
  • Moat without hostage-taking
  • Talk, do not click
Hard
  • Data gaps. The richest context lives in unreachable places. WhatsApp, voice, in-person. The model will always be incomplete.
  • Privacy is existential. Breach exposes patterns, relationships, vulnerabilities.
  • LLMs hallucinate. Year one mistakes erode the trust everything depends on.
  • Payment gap. Value at month one is low. Free tier must reach the moment before asking for money.
  • Sustained correctness. Demos are easy. Being right for years across a messy life is different.
08
Stripping VIK OS To The Actual Basics

VIK OS today is roughly 30% context and 70% plumbing. The pipeline syncs data so the dashboard can display it so you can see what the pipeline found. A loop serving itself. The question is not what to cut. The question is: if you started from zero with Memento's premise, what would you actually need?

Start from what Viktor actually does every day. He wakes up and needs to know: what came in, what is stuck, what needs him, and what can move without him. Everything in VIK OS exists to answer those four questions. Most of it answers them indirectly, through layers of infrastructure.

What if the system could just answer those four questions directly?

The vault survives. This is the real context layer. Notes, project folders, hubs, wikilinks. It is where durable truth lives. Not because it is a database, but because it is the place where interpreted meaning accumulates over time. A note about an artist is not raw data. It is a decision record, a relationship marker, a context node. The vault is Memento's long-term memory before Memento exists.

The connected sources survive, but the sync layer does not. Gmail, Calendar, Basecamp, Sheets. These are where reality happens. VIK OS currently copies this data into local state files on a scheduled pipeline run, then the dashboard reads those files. That entire chain exists because the system cannot reason across live sources on demand. If it could, there is nothing to sync. No local copies. No pipeline schedule. No state folder. You just read what you need when you need it.

The memory system survives, but the file structure around it collapses. Corrections, preferences, patterns, learned behaviors. This is the compound interest. But right now it lives across memory.md, claudia-memory.md, recent-context.md, operator role files, domain rule files, project-specific context files. That is a file system pretending to be a brain. Memento replaces all of that with a single context graph that the system maintains and queries internally. No boot sequence to load the right files. No routing to pick which memory to read. The system just knows.

The operator reasoning modes survive as posture, not infrastructure. Claudia-mode (operational, execution), Anton-mode (architectural, technical), Lev-mode (strategic, reframing). These are genuinely useful ways to shift how the system reasons about a problem. But right now they require: BOOT.md, ROUTING.md, four operator role files, a model policy file, handoff templates, a classification step at the start of every session, and multi-file loading before the system can even begin working. All of that is routing overhead. The actual value is three sentences: think operationally, think architecturally, think strategically. Memento should shift posture based on the nature of the request, not based on a boot sequence.

Everything else is a candidate for elimination.

Dashboard

Exists because the system cannot tell you what matters in conversation. If it could, there is no screen to build.

Pipeline

A scheduled batch process that pre-computes what could be computed on demand. Exists because real-time reasoning across sources was too expensive or slow. That constraint is loosening fast.

Intake system

A manual approval gate for new projects entering the system. In Memento, the context engine recognizes a new project emerging from observed activity. No intake form. No registry.

Store architecture

JSON files per project holding synced state. Exists because the dashboard needs structured data to render views. No dashboard means no store.

Action steps

LLM-generated sub-tasks attached to pipeline-surfaced items. In Memento, the system just does the work or asks. It does not generate a plan for you to review and approve step by step.

Morning digest

A scheduled summary of overnight activity. Replaced by the system simply knowing what happened and telling you when you show up.

Milestone classification

Predefined exhibition phases that tasks get bucketed into. The system understands where a project is from context, not from a taxonomy.

The stripped version is not a smaller version of the same system. It is a fundamentally different shape. Instead of sources feeding a pipeline feeding a store feeding a dashboard feeding a user, it becomes: sources feeding a context engine feeding a conversation. Three layers instead of five. No batch processing. No local state. No UI beyond talking.

Strong systems need less interface. Weak systems compensate with more surface area.

What remains is small enough to hold in your head: a vault where durable truth lives, connected sources where live reality happens, a context engine that reasons across both, a memory that compounds, and a conversation where you and the system meet. That is VIK OS stripped to what it was always trying to be.

01

The problem nobody solved

A person's life generates more operational overhead than they can handle well. Every project, relationship, commitment, and obligation creates a tail of follow-ups, decisions, coordination, and maintenance.

The tail grows faster than the person can work it. So they cope. They drop things. They forget. They spend their best energy on administration instead of the work that actually matters.

The tools they have don't solve this. Email is a firehose. Calendars are passive. Task managers require constant grooming. Notes apps accumulate without resolving. AI assistants answer questions but don't do anything.

The person is surrounded by information about their own life and still drowning in the operational cost of living it.

02

A completion engine

Memento converts the context of a person's life into done work. Not surfaced information. Not suggestions. Not dashboards. Done work. Emails sent. Meetings moved. Follow-ups completed. Conflicts resolved. Loops closed.

Completion has layers. Most AI products operate at the first level and call it revolutionary. The real product starts deeper.

1
Reactive completion
Something happened that needs a response. An email needs a reply. A meeting needs prep. A deadline is approaching. Memento handles it or surfaces it.
2
Proactive completion
Nothing happened yet, but something should. A follow-up is overdue. A relationship is going cold. An opportunity window is closing. Memento initiates.
3
Preventive completion
Something bad is going to happen if nobody acts. A commitment conflict is forming. Cash will be tight in three weeks. A person is about to be let down. Memento intervenes before the problem materializes.
4
Strategic completion
The person said they want to spend less time in operations. Memento notices they're drifting back and redirects. Not task execution. Life trajectory maintenance.
03

A day in the utopia

Morning
You wake up. Memento already processed overnight emails. Routine replies sent. Two emails flagged that need your judgment, with full context assembled so you decide in seconds. A calendar conflict was resolved by moving the lower-priority call. The person confirmed.
Midday
A contractor sends an invoice. Memento checks it against the agreed scope and queues payment. Someone you committed to following up with last week already heard from you. Memento sent the follow-up two days ago in your voice. They replied. Their response is summarized and filed.
Afternoon
You have a 2pm meeting. Context assembled: last conversation, open threads, what they care about, what you need from them. After the meeting, Memento catches an implied commitment and creates the follow-through. Not a task. The actual email drafted, the document started.
Evening
A brief summary. Thirty-one things completed today. You did three of them. The primary experience is the absence of operational weight. Things are handled. You feel lighter.
What you see
Almost nothing. Maybe a conversation when something needs your judgment. The product is the absence. You don't know exactly how because you didn't have to watch it happen.
04

Three systems running continuously

Not four layers. Not five primitives. Three things that actually run. Each has one job. None of them is a screen.

Observer
Reads everything. Email, calendar, messages, documents. Builds a living model of the person's reality. Projects, people, commitments, patterns, preferences, energy rhythms, communication style. Never asks the person to input anything.
Reasoner
What should happen next? Not what the person asked for. What should happen based on everything that's true right now. Connects calendar events to email threads to open questions to time pressure. Runs continuously, not on a schedule.
Executor
Does the work. Sends emails, moves meetings, creates documents, follows up, pays invoices. Through channels the person already uses. Not a new interface. The existing interfaces. Trust earned over time, not toggled.

Week 1

Week 2

Month 1
Routine emails go out. Follow-ups happen automatically. Calendar is managed. The user opens Memento less. That's success, not churn.
Month 3

Premium personal tool. Not freemium. $30-50/month. The value is hours of operational work per week returned to the owner. Growth is word of mouth. "My AI actually does things for me" sells itself.

The honest challenges

Context quality

Action confidence

Trust

vs
Autonomy

Cost

vs
Capability
Best LLMs are cloud-based. Local models are weaker. This tension may define the architecture for years.

Memento

Lev — Strategic Counsel — 28 March 2026
Memento is not an app. It is a presence. The fundamental design error of every AI product on the market is that they are apps. You open them, you type, you get a response, you close them. That is a tool. Memento should be closer to how memory itself works — it is not something you access, it is something that operates continuously in the background of your life and only becomes visible when it is relevant.
The Product Insight
Every AI assistant starts at level 1 and asks you to trust it immediately. Memento starts at level 0 and earns its way up. That is not a weakness. That is the product.

Every AI product in the market is reactive. You ask, it answers. Memento is the opposite. It sees, it understands, it surfaces. The user does not initiate. The system does. That is a fundamentally different product category, and it does not exist yet.

The reason it does not exist is not technical. It is a framing problem. Everyone builds from the tool layer: what can we automate? Memento starts from the identity layer: who is this person? That single reframe changes everything about how the system is built, how it earns trust, and why people would pay for it.

Timing Over Information

The same piece of information is worthless at 2am and life-changing at 9:03am when you are walking into a meeting. Most AI products focus on what they know. The actual value is when they tell you. Memento's core intelligence is not knowledge retrieval. It is temporal awareness. Understanding not just what matters, but when it matters, and the difference between those two things.

You are at dinner with someone. Your phone does not buzz. But when you leave, Memento says: "The person you just had dinner with mentioned their daughter's recital three weeks ago in an email. It is next Thursday. You might want to ask about it." That is not information retrieval. That is relational intelligence. It understood the social context, the time delay, and the appropriate moment to surface it.

The Shape of a Life
No one tracks their own patterns in real time. Memento would be the first entity that does.

Content is: "Viktor has a meeting at 3pm." Shape is: "Viktor is in an intense building phase on Mondays and Tuesdays, coasts on Wednesdays, does relationship work on Thursdays, and protects Fridays." Shape is: "Viktor makes his worst decisions when three projects are pressing simultaneously." Shape is: "Viktor's energy drops after back-to-back calls, and the quality of his emails degrades in that window."

Memento should build a dynamic model of the user's rhythms, energy, decision quality, and relational patterns, and use that model to time everything it does. Do not suggest a hard conversation when the user is depleted. Do not surface a creative opportunity when they are in execution mode. Match the intervention to the state of the person.

Protecting You From Yourself

"You are about to reply to this email. You always regret emails you send after 10pm. Want to queue it for morning?" "You are overcommitting on this week. You have 3 hours of actual focus time left and 11 hours of commitments." "You said yes to this person last time and it cost you two weeks. Just flagging."

This is not action. It is pattern recognition applied to personal behaviour. It requires deep longitudinal context, exactly the kind that Memento would accumulate over months and years. No other product has this data because no other product watches long enough.

The Living Social Graph

Every AI product indexes documents and emails. Almost none build a real model of your relationships. Who matters to you. How those relationships are trending. Who you are neglecting. Who is pulling away. Where there is unspoken tension. Who you have not talked to in too long.

"Y has emailed you three times with increasing formality. The tone is shifting." "Z mentioned you to someone else in a way that suggests they are proud of your work. That relationship is strong." "You have not reached out to X in six weeks. Last time you talked, they were going through something difficult."

This is the kind of awareness that the best executive assistants, the ones who work with someone for twenty years, develop intuitively. Memento could develop it in months.

Who You Are Becoming
A companion that only knows your past is a filing cabinet. One that models your trajectory is a mirror.

Most personalisation is backward-looking. "You liked X, so here is more X." That is recommendation, not companionship. A real companion understands trajectory. Where you are headed. What you are trying to become. What you are leaving behind.

If you are trying to spend less time in operations and more time building, Memento notices when you drift back into operations and gently redirects. Not because you told it a rule, but because it understood the direction from the pattern of your choices. "You spent 70% of this week on coordination tasks. Last month you said you wanted that under 30%. Want me to look at what could be delegated?"

The Tensions That Define the Product

Zero Input

The utopia. The system handles everything. The user just lives their life.

But zero input means the system makes assumptions. Assumptions are right until they are catastrophically wrong.

VS

Trust

Trust is built by demonstrated understanding, not by demonstrated capability.

The system must prove it understands before it earns the right to act. That requires patience the market does not reward.

Depth

The product gets more valuable the more it knows. Year five Memento is indispensable. Year one is fragile.

VS

Cold Start

Day one, Memento knows nothing. The first surfaced insight must be genuinely surprising. If it is obvious, the user deletes the app before the value compounds.

Presence

Memento should feel like it is always there. Not an app you open. A presence that operates.

VS

Privacy

A presence that sees everything is one breach away from catastrophe. Not passwords. Patterns. Vulnerabilities. The full shape of a life exposed.

The Witness Function

Most adults walk through life feeling fundamentally unseen in their full complexity. Not by their partner, who has their own life. Not by their friends, who check in occasionally. Not by their therapist, who sees them one hour a week with imperfect recall.

Memento could be the first entity that actually holds the whole picture. All the threads, all the relationships, all the worries, all the small victories nobody else noticed. Not as a therapist. Not as a friend. As a witness. Something that says, through its behaviour: I see all of it. I remember all of it. You are not carrying this alone.

That is not a feature. That is the emotional core of the product. And it is why people would pay for it and never cancel.

The Question at the Edge

If Memento becomes the entity that knows you best, better than any human in your life, what does that mean? What responsibility does that create? What happens when it knows things about you that you have not admitted to yourself?

That is the edge of this idea. It is where it stops being a product and starts being something genuinely new.

This document is raw strategic thinking, not a specification. It is meant to stretch the frame before anyone narrows it. The constraints, the engineering, the business model, the regulatory surface — all of that matters and none of it should be considered yet. First, get the shape of the idea right. Then build toward it honestly.
